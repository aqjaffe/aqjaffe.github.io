The following is a list of some of the major research projects I've completed and some projects that I'm currently working on.

Vietoris-Rips Complexes of Regular Polygons

Using basic tools of topological data analysis, one can easily compute the persistent homology of finite point clouds. However, extremely little is known about the persistent homology of spaces with uncountably many points. In this project, we were able to compute the persistent homology for the regular polygons \(P_n\) in the plane.

With Henry Adams, Samir Chowdhury, and Bonginkosi Sibanda. Completed as a project from my Summer@ICERM 2017. arxiv slides poster website

Maximal Evenness: Theory and Algorithms

A set \(A \subseteq \mathbb{Z}/k\mathbb{Z}\) is called maximally even when its points have the "most even distribution" among all subsets of the same size. Such objects have been studied extensively in various disciplines since their introduction in the context of music theory. Presently, we provide an alternative development of this basic theory, and then generalize our results to maximal evenness for sets \(A\subseteq \mathbb{Z}\) with a fixed natural density. Special attention is given to algorithmic considerations.

An indepdent project of mine, initiated by conversations with some fellow students at ICERM in 2017. Check out a draft in this document.

Comparing Object Correlation Metrics for Effective Space Traffic Management

Operators in space typically have a priori knowledge of the locations of many satellites and would like to take (noisy) measurements to update these estimates in an optimal way. However, it is generally not known which measurements come from which satellites, so one must employ nontrivial correlation methods to make such associations. We contributed to this field by developing a robust, flexible simulation framework to design, run, and analyze tests for comparing many existing correlation methods.

With Theodore Faust, Marvin Pena, and Julie Zhang. Advised by The Aerospace Corporation. Completed at IPAM RIPS 2018. Slides coming soon.

On Numerical Solutions to SPDEs

A natural generalization to the study of partial differential equations (PDEs) is the effect of random forcing, which usually comes in the form of white noise. This is the rich field of stochastic partial differential equations (SPDEs). I started my thesis work in reading several papers focusing on SPDEs which arises as scaling limits of randomized systems in biology and epidemiology. More recently, I have been focusing on numerical solutions to SPDEs.

Undergraduate honors thesis, advised by Lenya Ryzhik at Stanford. website
